Why I Decided to Become a Sysadmin

Published on 1 June 2026 in Career

For a while I wasn't sure which direction to take in tech. I enjoyed building things, but I kept bumping into the same realisation: whatever I built had to run on something, and that something was almost always Linux.

The penny-drop moment

Every cloud platform, every container, and every deployment pipeline I touched was sitting on top of a Linux machine somewhere. I could write the application code, but the moment something broke at the system level — a permission, a network setting, a service that refused to start — I was stuck. That gap is what pointed me toward systems administration.

Why start here

A mentor reframed my plan with one piece of advice: rather than chasing a "DevOps Engineer" title straight away, get into the industry as a Linux administrator first. The infrastructure fundamentals are the same, and they are the part that takes real time to build. DevOps and cloud work is, to a large extent, sysadmin skills with automation layered on top.

You can teach a tool in a week. You can't teach the instinct for why a system behaves the way it does — that only comes from running things and breaking them.

The plan from here

So the goal is set: become genuinely comfortable administering Linux, prove it with a recognised certification, and build the rest — automation, cloud, containers — on that foundation. The next post covers the certification I chose to anchor all of this.
